## What changed and why

This entry records the matcher change in 14544f4 (`perf: drop the alpha term on
the opaque linear scan`). The benchmark binary above is 351b950, which is
matcher-identical to 14544f4 (the commits between it and HEAD touch only docs,
the CLI flag refactor, and the research log — never applyNearest).

pixelize is faster than ImageMagick on every cell, in both wall and CPU time,
while staying exact: the "px vs ImageMagick diff" column is ImageMagick's own
non-nearest disagreement against pixelize's exact output (0.1% gameboy, 3.0% nes,
13.2% lego) — i.e. IM approximates and pixelize does not, and pixelize is still
~2-3x faster end-to-end.

### Our own matcher microbench (bench_test.go, best-of-6, this box)

Isolates the nearest-color matcher (no decode/resize/encode):

  exact path:  gameboy(P4) 15.3   pico8(P16) 29.1   nes(P64) 83.6   lego(P188,kd) 60.6  ns/px
  LUT path:    nes(P64) 12.5   lego(P188) 10.9  ns/px   (-lut/-lookup-table, P-independent)

The opaque alpha-drop (this commit) on the small-P exact path, measured against
the pre-change binary (best-of-6, parallel applyNearest):

  palette  P    before(4ch)  after(3ch)  speedup
  gameboy  4    18.43        17.04       1.08x
  pico8    16   37.63        31.07       1.21x
  nes      64   108.40       83.92       1.29x
  lego     188  59.41        60.22       ~1.00x  (kd path, untouched)

Bit-identical: the alpha-drop binary produced byte-for-byte identical PNGs to the
pre-change binary across 20 image x palette combinations on the demo set, and
TestParallelMatchesSerial confirms bit-identity vs the cp.Index oracle on random
noise. The LUT path is the opt-in approximate bulk mode (report 11); it is
P-independent and ~7x the exact small-P cost, which is why it only pays off when
the table is built once and reused (batch/watch).

Regression gate: exact paths report 0% non-nearest (golden + parallel + run-length
tests, race-clean); the LUT stays inside its ~2-6% accuracy budget
(TestFastLUTAccuracy).
